## Section 1: The Payoff of Spin-First Ordering

We have studied spin before angular momentum. This reverses the standard textbook presentation,
and we owe the reader an accounting for why.

The answer lies in what the reader now brings to this chapter. You have seen a physical phenomenon —
spin precession — that is discrete, quantized, and exhibits the strange 360-degree sign flip.
You have seen that this phenomenon requires SU(2) rather than SO(3) for its mathematical description.
You hold a concrete object: the spin-1/2 system, with its two states, its Pauli algebra, its
Larmor frequency.

Angular momentum, in its full generality, is the abstract case of which spin is the concrete instance.
The rotation group SO(3) — or more precisely, its covering group SU(2) — has many representations,
labeled by a non-negative integer or half-integer j. The j = 1/2 representation is spin-1/2.
The j = 1 representation gives the spin of a photon (or a classical angular momentum vector).
The j = 3/2 representation describes certain exotic particles.

Every representation gives a set of states with m = -j, -j+1, ..., j-1, j (2j+1 states total).
The j = 1/2 case: m = -1/2, +1/2 — the two spin states we have been studying. The j = 1 case:
m = -1, 0, +1 — three states. And so on.

This pattern — 2j+1 states per representation — is a theorem about the algebra of the rotation
group. It is not something we impose; it follows from the commutation relations [J_i, J_j] = i hbar epsilon_ijk J_k.

You already know the j = 1/2 version of this algebra. It is the Pauli algebra. The general
case is not harder in structure — it is the same algebra, generalized.

## Section 2: The Angular Momentum Algebra

The angular momentum algebra is generated by three operators J_x, J_y, J_z satisfying:

[J_i, J_j] = i hbar epsilon_ijk J_k

The Casimir element — the operator that commutes with all three J_i — is:

J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2

Because J^2 commutes with all three components, we can simultaneously diagonalize J^2 and
one component (conventionally J_z). The eigenvalues are:

J^2 |j, m> = hbar^2 j(j+1) |j, m>
J_z |j, m> = hbar m |j, m>

where j = 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2, ... and m = -j, -j+1, ..., j-1, j.

The raising and lowering operators J_+ = J_x + i J_y and J_- = J_x - i J_y satisfy:

J_+ |j, m> = hbar sqrt(j(j+1) - m(m+1)) |j, m+1>
J_- |j, m> = hbar sqrt(j(j+1) - m(m-1)) |j, m-1>

These ladder operators are why the eigenvalue ladder terminates: J_+ |j, j> = 0 (can't go
above m = j) and J_- |j, -j> = 0 (can't go below m = -j).

For j = 1/2, these reproduce the Pauli matrix algebra you already know:
J_x = hbar/2 sigma_x, J_y = hbar/2 sigma_y, J_z = hbar/2 sigma_z
with J_+ = hbar sigma_+ and J_- = hbar sigma_-.

The abstract algebra contains the concrete algebra as a special case. This is what spin-first
ordering enables: you can see the j = 1/2 case not as a mysterious special object but as
the simplest member of a coherent family.

## Section 3: Orbital Angular Momentum and the Connection to Space

Orbital angular momentum arises from the rotational symmetry of physical space. For a particle
with position r and momentum p, the orbital angular momentum is:

L = r × p

In quantum mechanics, p = -i hbar nabla, so:

L_x = y p_z - z p_y = -i hbar (y ∂/∂z - z ∂/∂y)
L_y = z p_x - x p_z = -i hbar (z ∂/∂x - x ∂/∂z)
L_z = x p_y - y p_x = -i hbar (x ∂/∂y - y ∂/∂x)

These satisfy the same commutation relations [L_i, L_j] = i hbar epsilon_ijk L_k. The
eigenfunctions of L^2 and L_z are the spherical harmonics Y_l^m(theta, phi), with eigenvalues:

L^2 Y_l^m = hbar^2 l(l+1) Y_l^m
L_z Y_l^m = hbar m Y_l^m

For orbital angular momentum, l must be a non-negative integer: l = 0, 1, 2, ... The
half-integer representations of the rotation algebra do not appear in the orbital case.
This is a theorem: the requirement that the spherical harmonics be single-valued functions
on the sphere forces l to be an integer.

Spin does not have this constraint. Spin is not a function on space; it is an internal degree
of freedom. The requirement of single-valuedness doesn't apply, and half-integer j values
(the j = 1/2 representation) are allowed.

This is the precise sense in which spin is a quantum phenomenon with no classical counterpart.
Classical orbital angular momentum has only integer representations. The half-integer representations
are intrinsically quantum.

  - Electron in hydrogen: L (orbital) + S (spin) = J (total). What are the allowed J values?

  - j1 ⊗ j2 = (j1+j2) ⊕ (j1+j2-1) ⊕ ... ⊕ |j1-j2|
  - Notation: |j1 j2; J M> = sum_{m1, m2} <j1 m1; j2 m2 | J M> |j1 m1> |j2 m2>

4.3 Examples:
  - 1/2 ⊗ 1/2 = 1 ⊕ 0 (triplet + singlet; this is the EPR state)
  - 1 ⊗ 1/2 = 3/2 ⊕ 1/2 (p-orbital electron with spin)

4.5 Physical meaning: why do the EPR particles form a singlet? The singlet state is the
one with total J = 0 — it has no preferred direction in space, which is what we mean by
"total spin zero." The non-locality of the EPR correlation is a property of the singlet state.

## Section 5: Fine Structure as Payoff [OUTLINE]

[This section needs to be written. Outline:

5.1 The hydrogen atom's energy levels from Bohr model: E_n = -13.6 eV / n^2.
  - These are degenerate in l and m for a given n.
  - Why does the real hydrogen atom have fine structure (slightly split energy levels)?
  
5.2 The spin-orbit coupling:
  - The electron orbiting the nucleus sees a magnetic field (from the nucleus's motion in
    the electron's rest frame). This magnetic field couples to the electron's spin.
  - The coupling term: H_SO = xi(r) L · S
  - L · S = (J^2 - L^2 - S^2) / 2, so its expectation value depends on J, l, and s.
  
5.3 The result: splitting of levels with the same n but different J values.
  - Classic example: 2p level splits into 2p_{1/2} and 2p_{3/2}.
  - The 2p_{1/2} to 1s transition and 2p_{3/2} to 1s transition give slightly different
    frequencies — this is observed in the sodium D-line doublet.
    
5.4 The payoff connection: this is why we needed CG coefficients. L · S evaluated in the
  |J M; l s> basis requires knowing how to decompose the direct-product state. The machinery
  of section 4 pays off here in a specific physical prediction.
  
5.5 Connection back to the book's central thesis: the fine structure is a consequence of
  spin, and spin is a consequence of the Lorentz group representation structure. Feynman
  said "If you want to know where the laws of physics come from, read [Weyl]." The fine
  structure of hydrogen is ultimately a consequence of the symmetry group of spacetime.
